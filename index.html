Understanding the Disease

So — what actually is Parkinson's?

Here's the honest, plain-language answer.

Deep in your brain is a small region called the substantia nigra — Latin for "black substance." It's named for the dark pigment in the neurons that live there. These neurons do one critical job: they make dopamine, a chemical messenger your brain uses to coordinate smooth, controlled movement.

In Parkinson's disease, these dopamine-producing cells slowly die off. Nobody fully knows why. As they disappear, the brain loses its ability to send clean movement signals to your muscles. The result? Tremors, stiffness, slowness, and eventually, much more.

"By the time most people are diagnosed, they've already lost 60–80% of their dopamine-producing neurons."

That's one of the most heartbreaking truths of this disease: it's often well underway before anyone notices. The brain compensates quietly, until it can't anymore.

Parkinson's is also more than a movement disorder. Sleep problems, depression, cognitive changes, and loss of smell can all precede the tremors by years — sometimes over a decade.

The substantia nigra — a small, darkly-pigmented region near the base of the brain — is where the damage begins. Neurons here produce dopamine, essential for fluid movement. In Parkinson's, they die faster than they should, and the brain loses its conductor.

Recognizing the Signs

It's more than a tremor.

The classic shaking hand is often what people picture — but Parkinson's announces itself in many other ways, some of which appear years before motor symptoms.

🤝
Tremor
A rhythmic shaking, often beginning in one hand at rest. The classic "pill-rolling" motion between thumb and finger. Usually one of the first visible signs.
Motor
🐢
Bradykinesia
Slowness of movement. Everyday tasks — buttoning a shirt, signing your name, getting up from a chair — become laborious. Steps get smaller. The face may lose expression.

🪨
Rigidity
Muscles stay stiff, limiting range of motion. Arms may not swing when walking. Neck and shoulders can ache. Rigidity can feel like exhaustion even after rest.

🫀
Loss of Smell
One of the most common early warning signs — often dismissed as a cold or allergy. A reduced ability to detect odors can appear a full decade before a diagnosis.

Sleep Disruption
REM sleep behavior disorder — physically acting out dreams — is strongly linked to PD. Some people unknowingly punch, kick, or shout during sleep years before any other symptom.

Depression, anxiety, and cognitive slowing are part of the disease — not just reactions to it. For many, these are among the most disabling aspects, yet they're often overlooked.

The State of the Science

What we know — and what we don't.

Honest science means acknowledging the gaps. Here's where the field stands.

✓  What We Know
Dopamine loss drives motor symptoms. When dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra die, movement becomes increasingly difficult to control.
A protein called alpha-synuclein plays a central role. In PD, it misfolds and clumps into "Lewy bodies" inside neurons — and those clumps appear to spread through the brain over time.
Genetics matter — but they're not destiny. Mutations in genes like LRRK2, SNCA, and Parkin increase risk. But most people with PD have no family history.
Environment is a factor. Pesticide exposure, head trauma, and industrial chemical exposure are all associated with elevated PD risk.
Levodopa remains the gold-standard treatment. It replaces depleted dopamine and manages symptoms — but loses effectiveness as the disease progresses and causes complications over time.
?  What We Still Don't Know
What triggers the disease in the first place? Even in genetic cases, most carriers never develop PD. The initial spark — why neurons begin dying in some people and not others — remains unknown.
How to stop it from progressing. Every treatment today manages symptoms. No drug, therapy, or intervention has been proven to slow, halt, or reverse the underlying neurodegeneration.
How to diagnose it early enough. There is no blood test, no brain scan, no biomarker that definitively confirms PD — especially in early stages, before most neurons are already gone.
Why it affects people so differently. Some live with manageable symptoms for decades; others deteriorate rapidly. We don't understand what drives these vastly different trajectories.
What role the gut-brain connection plays. Some evidence suggests PD may begin in the gut. The alpha-synuclein pathway and the gut microbiome are active areas of investigation — and largely unsolved.

Who Is Affected

Could it happen to you?

Parkinson's is often thought of as "an old person's disease." It's true that age is the biggest risk factor — most people are diagnosed after 60. But roughly 10-15% of cases are diagnosed before age 50, a group often called "young-onset" PD.

You don't have to have a family history. You don't have to show any particular symptoms early on. The disease can quietly progress for years — in your gut, your nose, your sleep — before it ever announces itself in your hands.

If your grandfather slowed down in his later years and you assumed it was "just aging," it may have been Parkinson's. If your parent was told they had depression or anxiety before the tremors started, that's also common. Parkinson's is underdiagnosed, undertreated, and under-recognized — and that's part of why this research matters.

Age
60+ years old — risk rises sharply. PD affects about 1 in 100 people over 60.
Sex
Men are 1.5× more likely to develop PD than women, though women often have a faster disease course.
Genetics
Family history raises risk — but 90%+ of PD cases have no clear genetic cause.
Environment
Pesticide and solvent exposure (e.g., paraquat, trichloroethylene) are among the strongest environmental risk factors identified.
Head Injury
Repeated traumatic brain injury has been linked to elevated PD risk — one reason the connection to contact sports is studied closely.

Our Role

Why our lab, and why now?

We exist to close the gap between what we know and what we need to know — through rigorous, specific, human-focused science.

Most labs pick a lane. Ours is the intersection of neuroscience, computational biology, and clinical translation — meaning we care equally about what's happening at the molecular level and whether it leads to something a patient can actually use.

We are motivated by the gap. We know a lot about what Parkinson's looks like, but far too little about how to stop it. We work on the hard problems: early biomarkers, disease mechanisms, and circuit-level disruptions that happen long before diagnosis.

Every experiment in this lab is aimed at one goal: shorten the time between the first neuron dying and the first person being helped. That is not a simple problem. It is the one worth solving.
